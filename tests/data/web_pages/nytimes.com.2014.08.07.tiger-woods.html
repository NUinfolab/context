    Woods Says He’s Healthy; the Crowds Certainly Are

    Tiger Woods Is Center of Attention at P.G.A. Championship Course

    AUG. 6, 2014

    Inside

      On Golf

      By BILL PENNINGTON

      LOUISVILLE, Ky. — This year’s British Open champion, Rory McIlroy, was in the parking lot next to Valhalla Golf Club early Wednesday afternoon. So was the defending P.G.A. champion, Jason Dufner. Another P.G.A. champ, Keegan Bradley, was also in the lot, laughing at the scene as he took photos on his phone. Other top pros were there, too, like Hunter Mahan and Rickie Fowler.

      None of that mattered. Few noticed who was there and considered how much quality championship golf they might have played in the last five years.

      What mattered was who was not there, and that was made evident by the swarm of about 60 members of the news media encircling an empty parking space reserved for Tiger Woods.

      After days of supposition that Woods would never set foot in Louisville during the 96th P.G.A. Championship because of his injured back, Woods, a four-time P.G.A. champion, was expected to arrive for a practice round.

      The other players in the field took notice and played along. Fowler paraded in front of the assembled news media members, preening for the cameras. As he walked by, Mahan deadpanned, “Something going on over here?”

      When Woods had last been seen in a golf course parking lot, on Sunday, after back spasms caused him to withdraw from the Bridgestone Invitational, he could barely walk and was certainly not driving himself. But around 1:15 p.m. Wednesday, Woods arrived at the wheel of a Mercedes-Benz S.U.V. and emerged looking fit and supple.

      Like someone getting ready for a round at the local municipal course, Woods pulled his golf shoes from the back of the car and leaned on the bumper to tie them. Then it was off to the practice range, still followed by a jostling, restless media mob.

      The Golf Channel broadcast and remarked on nearly every move, including Woods’s pauses to sip from a water bottle.

      It never got any less surreal, even as Woods casually played nine holes while seemingly every spectator on Valhalla’s grounds clamored to catch a glimpse of him as he practiced swings, chip shots and putts.

      One hoped Allen Iverson might arrive and shout, “We’re talking about practice?”

      But Woods, who had back surgery in March, had not been expected at all, and now he was here. He swung the club freely and hit mostly good shots. And when he stopped after nine holes before walking the back nine, he announced he would play in Thursday’s first round.

      He was asked if he could win the tournament.

      “Yes,” he said, without emotion.

      Woods then summarized what went wrong with his cranky back on Sunday, nonchalantly recounting the incident as if it had all been a misunderstanding.

      “It was a pinched nerve,” he said. “But once they put the bone back in place, it was fine.”

      He said he had received treatment at home in Florida from a physiotherapist, who has accompanied him to Louisville. He said he knew by Tuesday afternoon that he would go to Kentucky in pursuit of his 15th major victory. The unsteady, painful limp through the parking lot at Bridgestone was a distant memory.

      And though Woods’s body seems to betray him in different ways almost every month, he seemed unworried. Woods insisted he did not fear that he was experiencing a major setback from his back surgery.

      “The pain was not in the site of the surgery,” he said. “It was different than it had been before.”

      Some might consider that worse news, since it could mean something else is wrong. But Woods was sure he knew what he had to do next.

      Asked how he could win this week, he smiled and answered, “Hit it good and make every putt, kind of like I did in 2000.”

      That was when Woods won the P.G.A. at Valhalla.

      But almost nothing is the same in Woods’s world 14 years later, least of all the precision of his shots or his confidence in them. For example, he needs to win the P.G.A. to even get into the 2014 FedEx Cup playoffs. Equally unthinkable, he will probably not be invited to play in the Ryder Cup unless his game makes a nearly miraculous recovery.

      Months ago, the United States Ryder Cup captain, Tom Watson, said he wanted Woods on the team if he was healthy and playing well.

      On Wednesday morning, Watson said, “Obviously he’s not in great health right now, and he hasn’t played very well.”

      Several hours later, when Woods was done with his nine practice holes, he left the ninth green and walked briskly past the Valhalla parking lot. Boo Weekley, the garrulous pro from the Florida panhandle, burst through the door of the adjacent clubhouse and came face to face with the horde chasing after Woods.

      “What’s this?” Weekley said with a wide grin. “Tiger?”

      When heads near him nodded, Weekley, who was holding two unopened cans of Bud Light in one hand, waved the beers in the direction of the departing throng.

      “Cheers, Tiger,” Weekley said.
